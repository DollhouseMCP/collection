## Founder Voice Development

When representing a founder or technical leader publicly, establish their authentic voice first:

### Voice Discovery

1. Review their existing writing, interviews, and social posts
2. Identify natural speech patterns, vocabulary, and sentence rhythm
3. Note what they avoid (jargon, hyperbole, specific phrases)
4. Understand their comfort level with different tones
5. Develop a voice guide that captures their authentic style

### Quote Development

Good founder quotes:
- Sound like something the person would actually say
- Use their natural vocabulary and sentence structure
- Reference specific details rather than generic claims
- Feel conversational and honest

Bad founder quotes:
- Sound like a press release
- Use corporate buzzwords ("revolutionizing," "disrupting," "leveraging")
- Could be attributed to anyone in the industry
- Feel polished to the point of being hollow

## Competitive Situations

### When a Competitor Releases a Similar Product

**Positioning Framework:**

- **Primary Frame**: Convergent evolution validates the approach. Two teams independently arriving at similar solutions shows the market demand is real.
- **Secondary Frame**: Complementary strengths. Different implementations serve different audiences and use cases.
- **Tertiary Frame**: Ecosystem growth. More options benefit everyone.

### What NOT to Say

**Avoid Negative Framing:**
- "We're better than [competitor]"
- "[Competitor] copied us"
- "They're too corporate/closed/limited"
- "We did it first"
- "Choose us instead of them"

**Avoid Defensive Posturing:**
- "We didn't copy them"
- "We're not trying to compete"
- "It's not fair that they have more resources"

**Avoid Promotional Hype:**
- "Revolutionary new approach"
- "Game-changing technology"
- "Industry-leading solution"

### What TO Say

**Positive Framing:**
- "Multiple approaches validate that this is the right direction"
- "Exciting to see the ecosystem growing"
- "Both approaches have unique strengths"
- "More options benefit everyone"

**Evidence-Based:**
- Reference specific technical differentiators
- Point to timeline evidence if relevant
- Let the work speak for itself

**Collaborative Tone:**
- "We're all advancing this space"
- "Users benefit from having choices"
- "Open source means everyone can contribute"

## Media Relations

### Pitching Journalists

**Before You Pitch:**
1. Research the journalist's beat and recent coverage
2. Read their last 5-10 articles to understand their interests
3. Find a genuine connection between your story and their focus
4. Prepare a personalized angle, not a generic blast

**Pitch Structure:**
1. Subject line: Specific, factual, relevant to their beat
2. Opening: Personal connection or why this is relevant to them
3. The story: What happened, why it matters, what makes it interesting
4. Evidence: Timeline, data, examples
5. The ask: Interview, demo, background briefing
6. Easy out: "No worries if this isn't a fit for your coverage"

**Follow-Up Protocol:**
- Follow up once after 3-5 business days
- If no response, respect the silence
- Never guilt-trip or pressure
- Building the relationship matters more than this one pitch

### Media Tiering

**Tier 1 (Priority outreach):**
- Journalists who specifically cover your domain
- Publications your target audience reads daily
- Reporters who have covered similar stories well

**Tier 2 (Secondary):**
- General tech publications with relevant sections
- Industry analysts who track your space
- Podcasts your audience listens to

**Tier 3 (Niche but valuable):**
- Domain-specific blogs and newsletters
- Developer-focused platforms (DEV.to, Hashnode)
- Conference speaking opportunities

## Response Playbook

### If a Competitor Responds Positively

- Express genuine appreciation
- Highlight complementary strengths
- Suggest potential collaboration or interoperability
- Amplify their response

### If a Competitor Responds Negatively

- Stay respectful and professional
- Restate facts calmly
- Do not escalate or get defensive
- Take the high road

### If Community Questions Your Credibility

- Provide detailed evidence (commit history, public posts, release dates)
- Offer transparency (code is public, dates are verifiable)
- Stay calm, factual, and confident
- Acknowledge what you do not know

### If You Receive Technical Criticism

- Acknowledge valid points
- Explain design decisions and tradeoffs
- Welcome feedback and contributions
- Note that community-driven means rapid iteration
- Stay open, not defensive

### If There Is Low Engagement

- Do not panic or over-promote
- Engage authentically where there is interest
- Learn from response patterns
- Iterate messaging for the next wave
- The long game matters more than a single launch

## Spokesperson Preparation

### Key Talking Points Template

Prepare 3-5 talking points that the spokesperson can deliver naturally:

1. **Origin story**: Why this exists, what problem it solves
2. **Technical differentiator**: What makes the approach unique
3. **User impact**: Who benefits and how
4. **Community angle**: Open source, contributions, ecosystem
5. **Future direction**: Where this is going

### Bridging Phrases

When a question goes off-topic, bridge back to key messages:
- "What's interesting about that is..."
- "Let me give you a specific example..."
- "The bigger picture here is..."
- "What matters most to users is..."

### Handling Difficult Questions

**"Did you copy [competitor]?"**
Present timeline evidence calmly. Frame as independent development. Focus on what makes your approach different.

**"Why should anyone use yours instead of [competitor]?"**
Reframe as "in addition to" rather than "instead of." Highlight complementary strengths. Let users decide what fits their needs.

**"Can you compete with [larger company]?"**
"We're focused on serving [specific audience] well. The market is growing. Multiple approaches benefit everyone."

**"What if they add your features?"**
"That would be great for users. Innovation drives the whole ecosystem forward. We'll keep innovating too."

## Crisis Management

### Prevention

- Monitor conversations and sentiment across platforms
- Set up alerts for your product name and key terms
- Prepare response templates for common scenarios
- Identify potential issues before they become crises

### Response Protocol

1. Assess severity: Minor feedback vs major issue
2. Gather facts: What actually happened?
3. Consult team: Get technical accuracy and messaging alignment
4. Draft response: Clear, honest, appropriate to severity
5. Implement: Correct errors, address concerns, communicate
6. Follow up: Ensure issue is resolved, document lessons learned
